What is an Inductor?
Basic Explanation & Working Principle

An inductor is a fundamental passive electronic component that stores energy in a magnetic field when electric current flows through it. It is an essential building block in power electronics, radio frequency (RF) circuits, and noise filtering.

1 How Does an Inductor Work?

An inductor typically consists of a coil of insulated wire wound around a core. When an electric current flows through the wire, it creates a magnetic field around the coil. This process is based on Ampere's Law.

Understanding Inductance (L)

The property of an inductor to oppose any change in the electric current flowing through it is called Inductance. According to Lenz's Law, the inductor creates a "back-EMF" (electromotive force) to fight the change in current, making it a stable energy reservoir.

The strength of this inductance is measured in Henries (H). The value is determined by the number of turns in the coil, the diameter of the coil, and the magnetic permeability of the core material.

2 Common Types of Inductors

Air Core Inductors

Wound around a non-magnetic form. Ideal for high-frequency applications like RF circuits because they avoid core losses.

Ferrite Core Inductors

Common in switch-mode power supplies (SMPS). Ferrite provides high permeability and low energy loss at high frequencies.

Iron Core Inductors

Used for low-frequency applications (e.g., 50/60Hz power transformers). They provide very high inductance but are heavier.

Toroidal Inductors

Doughnut-shaped cores that keep the magnetic field contained, significantly reducing interference (EMI) with other components.

3 Where are Inductors Used?

Inductors are vital in modern electronics for controlling current and frequency:

  • Power Filtering: Inductors act as "chokes" to block high-frequency noise (AC) while letting pure DC pass through to sensitive components.
  • Tuning Circuits: Combined with capacitors, they form LC Circuits used to select specific radio frequencies.
  • DC-DC Converters: Essential in Boost and Buck converters to store energy and step voltage up or down.
  • Transformers: By using two coupled inductors, energy can be transferred between circuits via mutual induction.
